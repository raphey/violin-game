# Violin Ear Training Game — Pedagogical Rationale

Each category and level is designed to build ear training skills progressively, from simple pitch recognition to melodic pattern identification. The game always uses real violin audio and asks the player to echo what they hear — the goal is to train the ear, not just the fingers.

---

## Open Strings

**Notes used:** G3, D4, A4, E5

The open strings are the first sounds a violinist learns to produce reliably. Starting here grounds the player's ear in the four foundational pitches of the instrument before any left-hand technique is required.

### Level 1 — Single whole notes
Each question is one open string, held for four beats. The only task is to identify and reproduce a single pitch. This builds the fundamental skill of matching a tone by ear with no rhythmic complexity.

---

## See-Saw

**Notes used:** A4, B4, E5

These three notes are the core of the first Suzuki Book 1 piece ("Song of the Wind" warm-up / "See-Saw"). They live on the A string and are typically among the first left-hand notes a student learns.

### Level 1 — Repeated half notes (same note twice)
Two identical half notes. The player hears a pitch and repeats it. This isolates pitch matching from interval recognition — the challenge is sustain and accuracy, not distinguishing between notes.

### Level 2 — Two half notes (same or different)
Any combination of A4, B4, and E5 in two half notes. Now the player must identify which note they heard and distinguish between the three pitches. Intervals include unisons, seconds (A–B), and fifths (A–E, B–E).

### Level 3 — Three notes with mixed rhythms
Three notes from {A4, B4, E5} in combinations of quarter and half notes (♩♩𝅗𝅥, ♩𝅗𝅥♩, 𝅗𝅥♩♩). Adds rhythmic complexity alongside pitch, requiring the player to track both duration and pitch simultaneously.

### Level 4 — Four quarter notes
Four quarter notes from {A4, B4, E5}. The fastest rhythmic subdivision in the game so far. Tests whether the player can identify and reproduce a four-note melodic fragment in time.

## Twinkle

**Notes used:** A4, B4, C#5, D5, E5, F#5

"Twinkle Twinkle Little Star" introduces the full A major scale on the A string (A4 through F#5). This is the first category with a raised seventh (G#, implied by key) and the characteristic scale passages of the Suzuki repertoire.

### Level 1 — A4 paired with each scale degree
A4 is always the first note; the second note is one of B4, C#5, D5, E5, F#5. This trains interval recognition upward from the tonal center A, covering seconds through sixths.

### Level 2 — Repeated half notes on each scale degree
Two identical half notes on each pitch from A4 to F#5. Similar to See-Saw Level 1 but expanding the pitch set — the player must reliably distinguish all six pitches on the A string.

### Level 3 — Two half notes, melodic pairs from the piece
Pairs drawn from significant melodic intervals in Twinkle: A–E (fifth), E–F# (second), F#–E (second descending), E–D (second descending), D–C# (second descending), C#–B (second descending), B–A (second descending). Mirrors the actual melodic motion of the piece.

### Level 4 — Three notes with mixed rhythms
Three-note fragments with rhythmic variation (♩♩𝅗𝅥, ♩𝅗𝅥♩, 𝅗𝅥♩♩). Draws from melodic cells in Twinkle (e.g., A A E, E F# E, D C# B). Bridges rhythm and melodic memory.

### Level 5 — Four quarter notes
Four-note cells drawn from the actual melodic content of Twinkle (e.g., A A E E, E E F# F#, F# F# E E). Full rhythmic and melodic complexity using all six pitches.

## Lightly Row

**Notes used:** A4, B4, C#5, D5, E5

"Lightly Row" uses a pentatonic-ish subset of the A major scale and features a mix of stepwise motion and thirds. It introduces the key interval of a third (e.g., A–C#, B–D, C#–E) prominently.

### Level 1 — E5 paired with each scale degree (descending approach)
E5 is always the first note; the second note is A4, B4, C#5, D5, or E5. Trains interval recognition downward from E5, emphasizing how each note relates to the top of the phrase.

### Level 2 — Two half notes, thirds and steps from the piece
Pairs reflecting the melodic intervals in Lightly Row: thirds (A–C#, B–D, C#–E and inversions) and steps (B–C#, C#–D, D–E). Directly trains the ear on the harmonic language of the piece.

### Level 3 — Three notes with mixed rhythms
Three-note fragments centered on thirds (A–C#–A, C#–A–C#, B–D–B, etc.) with rhythmic variation. Builds fluency with the characteristic interval of the piece in both directions.

### Level 4 — Four quarter notes
Dense four-note combinations drawn from the full pitch set of Lightly Row. Represents the highest complexity level for this category — all intervals from the piece, in varied order.

## Song of the Wind

**Notes used:** A4, B4, C#5, D5, E5, F#5, A5

"Song of the Wind" is centered around A but visits D as a secondary tonal center. The pedagogical structure of the first two levels is designed to internalize the sound of every note in the song *relative to each tonal center*, so the player hears the music from two harmonic perspectives.

### Level 1 — All notes heard relative to A (tonal center: A)
A4 is always the first note. The second note is B4, C#5, D5, E5, F#5, or A5. The player hears every note in the song as an interval above A — the home pitch. This trains the ear to hear each scale degree in relation to the tonic, grounding the song harmonically before learning the melody.

### Level 2 — All notes heard relative to D (tonal center: D)
D5 is always the first note. The second note is A4, B4, C#5, E5, F#5, or A5. D is a prominent secondary tonal center in Song of the Wind, and the song's phrasing often resolves to or departs from D. Hearing all other notes relative to D develops a richer harmonic understanding of the piece — the player learns to feel both "home" pitches of the song.

### Level 3 — Triad pairs (A major and D major)
Half note pairs drawn from the A major triad {A4, C#5, E5} and the D major triad {D5, F#5, A5} — all 12 ordered pairs without repetition. These two triads together cover all the notes in Song of the Wind and represent its two tonal centers harmonically. Training on these pairs builds the ability to hear intervals within each chord, reinforcing the harmonic skeleton of the piece.

### Level 4 — Consecutive melodic pairs from the song
Half note pairs covering every consecutive interval that appears in Song of the Wind (melody: A B C# D E F# D A F# E D C# B A C# E), deduplicated. All 15 unique consecutive pairs are included. This level connects the abstract interval training of the earlier levels directly to the melodic context of the piece — the player is now hearing and reproducing the actual musical transitions they will encounter when playing the song.
